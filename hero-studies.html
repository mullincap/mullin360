ADepth of field

Throw the parking lot out of focus.

The photograph's real problem is that its bottom half is in sharp focus on parked cars. No cinematographer would ever do that. A single masked backdrop-filter blurs, desaturates, and darkens the lower frame on a gradient — zero at the horizon, full at the bottom edge. The cars become atmosphere. The sunset and Camelback stay sharp and rich, and the eye goes where it belongs.

  • Blur 7px, saturation 45%, brightness 86% — gradient-masked to the lower frame only
  • An offset vignette, brightest at the mountain rather than a lazy dark border
  • No new assets, no JS, ~12 lines of CSS
  • Nobody consciously notices it — they just think the photo is better than it is

The cheapest real fix. If you only take one thing from this page, take this one.

BThe aperture

Lead with the only beautiful two inches.

The frame opens from a cinematic letterbox — the sunset and Camelback's silhouette, the one genuinely good part of this photograph — and the aperture expands to full over two and a half seconds. Two seal-green rules ride the opening edges and fade. You see the strongest imagery first, and the reveal reads as a title sequence rather than a slideshow. Depth of field fades in behind it.

  • clip-path inset, on the brand easing — nothing bounces or spins
  • Hold the letterbox a beat, then open: the sunset is the first thing anyone sees
  • The image settles from 1.14 → 1.0 as it opens, so the land seems to relax into place
  • Under prefers-reduced-motion it renders as the graded still, no animation

The signature moment. Costs nothing, and it's the thing someone would screenshot.

CThe seal grade

Grade it into the brand.

Everything in A, plus the photograph is graded into the palette rather than merely dimmed. A gradient multiply pulls the shadows toward night-green — biting the darks while leaving the sky intact — and a cool bone soft-light lifts the highlights without sepia. The red LED roofline is muted by the desaturation, though honestly it cannot be surgically removed in CSS: it shares its hue with the sunset, and killing one kills the other. A 42-second drift keeps the frame quietly alive.

  • Shadows → #0C120D on a gradient multiply; highlights → bone via soft-light
  • Desaturation to 78% mutes the red without draining the sky
  • An endless, almost imperceptible drift — the frame breathes instead of freezing
  • The most "designed" of the three, and the furthest from the source photo

The most authored — and the most opinionated. Push it too far and the sunset turns to mud; this is tuned to the edge of that.

→Recommendation

A and B, together.

Depth of field fixes the content of the photograph; the aperture gives you a moment worth remembering and leads with its best material. They compose without conflict, and neither one editorialises the image so hard that it stops looking like Scottsdale. C is the most beautiful frame on this page, but it is also the one where a client might say "that isn't what it looks like."

All three are pure CSS on the photograph you already have. No new assets, no libraries, no build step — and every one of them degrades to a still, graded frame under prefers-reduced-motion.
